// Synopsis

Drone Commander is a hybrid Visual Novel / RTS / RPG game that dynamically alternates between gameplay modes to keep the experience varied and cinematic. Players engage in story-driven conversations, tactical real-time strategy battles, and explore RPG-style sections with character interaction and minigames.

The player, an ordinary human, is abducted into a galactic conflict involving several alien factions and an ominous, destructive Type 3 drone fleet. Initially seen as primitive, the player gradually gains influence by showing an uncanny ability to control drones and coordinate defense strategies.

The player's fleet, called the Vanguard, is on the run from the drone fleet while rescuing alien races and occasionally launching counterattacks. Humanity has only recently begun to advance technologically after surviving a near-catastrophic drone attack. Characters have rich backstories and personality-driven arcs, and the choices you make shape alliances, friendships, rivalries, and even romance.

Gameplay Modes

  • VN Mode: Storytelling, dialogue trees, choices with consequences
  • RTS Mode: Command fleets, deploy drones, manage shields, formations, and superweapons
  • RPG Mode: Explore ships and alien planets, interact with crew, complete quests, and engage in pixel-art shooter combat
  • Minigames: Crew hiring, job assignment, research progression, morale tracking, fleet economy management

Tone & Themes

A balance of emotional storytelling, sci-fi mystery, and tactical warfare. Explores themes of identity, trust between species, power, and redemption. Blends anime-style drama with strategic gameplay and rich lore.

// Gameplay

RTS — Base Building Guide

Welcome, Commander. Space strategy games have a terrain problem. Without hills, chokepoints, or rivers, battles devolve into blobs smashing into blobs in an empty void. Asteroids only get you so far.

Drone Commander does something different. Instead of importing ground-war mechanics into space and hoping they stick, we built a strategy layer native to orbital combat — one where the battlefield has structure, territory, and depth without a single wall or turret line. Everything you build, everything you fight over, is rooted in the science-fiction of the universe itself.

This guide walks you through how base building works, what you’re managing, and why it all matters.

Your Fleet: Three Ships, One War

You don’t build a base in Drone Commander. You deploy a fleet. Your operations run through three capital ships, each with a distinct role. Think of them as your economy, your brain, and your fist.

The Command Ship

This is your nerve centre. Everything starts here. When you deploy the command ship at the start of a mission, your first move is to fire up the Nanite Forges — internal reactors that passively generate energy. You’ll start with three, and they’ll quietly tick away, filling your energy reserves while you figure out your opening strategy.

Energy is the gating resource for everything else. You spend it to unlock departments — the four operational branches that open up your options:

  • Mining Operations — unlocks harvesters (for ore), enticers (for preparing void matter extraction), and void harvesters
  • Engineering & Prospecting — unlocks surveyors (for research points) and the ability to research drift projectors
  • Defence Industry — lets you mount turrets, shield generators, and other defensive systems directly onto your ships
  • Cyber Warfare — opens up a suite of offensive and defensive digital attacks, but it’s the most expensive department to enable, making it a deliberate mid-to-late game commitment

You can’t unlock everything at once. At roughly 10 energy per second from three forges, your first department takes about 20 seconds to come online. That’s 20 seconds of defending with whatever you arrived with while you decide: do I go for economy first, or territory, or defences?

That tension never goes away.

The Research Ship

Your upgrade platform. The research ship doesn’t build combat units — it makes everything else better. Fleet-wide damage boosts, armour upgrades, engine speed, harvesting efficiency, drift field improvements — it’s all here, and it all costs research points.

The research ship is also where you unlock the blueprints for advanced units like the Dampener Ship and the entire cyber warfare tech tree.

Every RP you spend here is an RP you’re not spending somewhere else. Want to go all-in on cyber warfare? That’s 265 RP for the full tree — RP that could have gone to weapons upgrades, drift field efficiency, or dampener blueprints. The research ship is where your long-term strategy takes shape.

The Assault Carrier

Your war factory. The assault carrier builds all your combat ships — fighters, raiders, beam ships, missile frigates, battleships, shield ships, and heavier capital-class vessels like the Arkhelion.

Before you can build most unit types, you need to research them on the assault carrier (which costs energy), then deploy a construction field — a platform within a set radius of the carrier where your units are actually assembled. You also set a rally point so completed ships automatically disembark clear of the build zone.

The actual building is handled by constructors — small utility ships that pick up ore and energy from the command ship, fly over to the construction field, and spend those resources assembling the unit. Fighters are the exception — they don’t need constructors and can be produced directly.

As the game progresses, you can unlock additional construction fields to increase your throughput. More fields mean faster fleet replacement, which matters when you’re losing ships in a contested drift zone and need reinforcements now.

The Economy: Four Resources, One Loop

Energy

Generated passively by nanite forges on the command ship. Used to unlock departments and research unit types. You don’t need to micromanage it — the forges tick away on their own — but you do need to decide when to spend your reserves, because unlocking a department at the wrong time means you’re sitting without energy when something more urgent comes up.

Ore

The workhorse resource. Ore funds almost every unit and structure in the game. Harvesters fly out from the command ship to mining zones near the planet below, descend to the surface, collect ore, and return to deposit it. The mining zones drift across the map as the planet orbits, so your harvesters are constantly adjusting their routes — you can’t just park them and forget.

Research Points (RP)

Generated by surveyors — ships you build at the command ship and then deploy to research zones scattered across the map. Once a surveyor is in position, it passively generates about 1 RP per second by scanning the local environment. You can station up to two surveyors per zone.

RP is the currency of strategic choice. It buys drift projector research, cyber warfare unlocks, fleet-wide upgrades, and advanced unit blueprints. You never have enough, and every spend closes a door somewhere else.

Void Matter

The most interesting resource in the game — and the one that ties everything back to the lore.

To get void matter flowing, you first build an enticer and send it to a null field on the map. The enticer prepares the null field for harvesting (about 30 seconds). Once it’s ready, you deploy void harvesters to extraction points within the field, and they begin pulling void matter.

Void matter is what sustains your drift fields — the territorial hex network that forms the backbone of your defence. Every active drift projector drains about 1 void matter per second. Run out, and your fields start going dark. This means void matter income isn’t optional — it’s the lifeblood of your territorial presence.

Drift Fields: Your Terrain in Space

This is the heart of Drone Commander’s RTS identity.

The Problem

In a ground-based RTS, terrain does the heavy lifting for strategic depth. Mountains funnel armies. Rivers create chokepoints. Walls and turrets form layered defences that attackers must chew through. None of that exists in space.

The Solution

The entire map is divided into a honeycomb grid of hexagonal zones. At the start of a mission, this grid is invisible. When you build and deploy a drift projector into a hex cell, that cell activates — visually distinct, owned by you, and projecting altered physics within its boundaries.

Inside a friendly drift field, your ships gain a combat advantage: increased speed and increased firepower. Inside an enemy drift field, your ships are debuffed. The fields don’t block movement — any ship can fly into any hex — but the further you push into enemy territory, the more the odds stack against you.

This is what replaces walls and turrets. One enemy drift field is manageable. Three in a row, each stacking debuffs on your fleet while buffing theirs, is a meatgrinder. You’re not breaking through a gate — you’re fighting uphill through increasingly hostile physics.

Chains and Networks

Drift projectors don’t work in isolation. Every active projector must trace an unbroken chain of friendly hexes back to your command ship. The command ship is the power source. If a projector in the middle of your chain gets destroyed, everything beyond it goes dark — no buff, no debuff, just neutral space.

This is where the real strategy lives. A wide, shallow network is resilient but offers weaker coverage. A narrow, deep one projects power but is brittle — one surgical strike on a junction hex and half your network collapses. You’re constantly making decisions about network shape, and the enemy is always looking for the load-bearing hex to knock out.

Managing the Grid

Each drift projector has a manual on/off toggle. Mid-battle, you might shut down rear hexes to conserve void matter for the front line, or go dark on a flank to consolidate resources for a decisive push. You can also reclaim any drift projector — destroying it instantly and recovering some void matter — to reposition your network on the fly.

Breaking Through

When attacking into enemy drift fields, you’ll want a Dampener Ship — a specialist unit that reduces the debuff effects of enemy drift fields within a radius around itself. Dampener ships require significant RP investment to unlock (70 RP across two research tiers), but without them, pushing through deep enemy networks is brutal.

The Science Behind the Strategy

Here’s what makes Drone Commander’s base building more than just mechanics.

Strange Matter and the Ancient Gold Rush

Billions of years before the events of the game, ancient races noticed something extraordinary: the Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation — the echo of the Big Bang — wasn’t perfectly uniform. In twelve locations across the galaxy, CMBR appeared to clump together. When they investigated, they discovered that in these regions, the fundamental constants of the universe were different. The speed of light, the permittivity of space, the values that define how reality works — all variable.

What they found was a substance they called strange matter — not the quark-based strange matter of human physics, but something far more profound. This matter allowed direct manipulation of spacetime itself.

A galaxy-wide gold rush followed. Every advanced civilisation raced to these twelve locations to claim what they could. They refined the strange matter into shards — portable batteries that could locally alter the fundamental constants of the universe. With a shard, you could change the speed limit. You could make distances shorter. You could rewrite the rules.

Starlanes and Null Fields

The ancients used their shards to build starlanes — corridors across the galaxy where spacetime behaves differently, allowing travel between star systems at speeds that would otherwise be impossible. Different shards had different capabilities, and the starlane network became the infrastructure of galactic civilisation.

Those ancient races are long gone — wiped out by the Drone Fleet or vanished into history. But the starlanes remain. And where starlanes exist, were planned, or have degraded, they leave behind null fields — ghostly residue where the strange matter that once warped spacetime has slowly leaked its energy.

Void Matter: Bottled Leftovers

Void matter is what you harvest from null fields. It’s the waste product — the faint, degraded trickle of the original strange matter that once powered starlane corridors. It retains just enough of the source material’s properties to allow localised manipulation of physical constants.

When you deploy a drift projector and it creates a hex field, what’s actually happening is a crude, small-scale recreation of what the ancients did with shards. You’re projecting a zone of altered fundamental constants into the battlefield. Your ships perform better inside because the physics are literally different there — tuned in your favour.

This is why drift fields cost void matter to sustain. You’re actively maintaining a region of warped spacetime, burning through the resource to keep the constants altered. Stop feeding it void matter, and reality snaps back to normal.

The Bigger Picture

The entire RTS economy mirrors what’s happening at the galactic scale. The Drone Fleet — the Dark Worlders — strip-mine entropy (crystallised, locked information — essentially the soul of living beings) to power their cosmic infrastructure, including the ability to cast fake universes and import exotic matter. You’re doing the same thing at a tactical level: harvesting the remnants of ancient technology to project zones of altered physics that give you a fighting chance.

The resource hierarchy reflects the civilisation tiers of the galaxy:

  • Void Matter — accessible to all spacefaring races. The dregs. Enough to project basic drift fields
  • Resonant Essence — accessible to the Vionites and above. Discovered after 300 years of pulsar research. Stronger effects
  • Entropy — accessible to sovereign-tier civilisations. The real power source. What the Dark Worlders harvest from the living
  • Arch-Matter — accessible only to the most ancient races. The stuff of legends

Every resource you spend in Drone Commander has a lore reason for existing, a lore reason for being scarce, and a lore reason for doing what it does. The strategy isn’t layered on top of the fiction — it grows out of it.

Cyber Warfare: The Invisible Front

Once you’ve stabilised your economy and drift field network, the Cyber Department opens up an entirely different axis of attack.

Cyber operations are targeted actions with cooldowns, launched from the command ship after enabling the Cyber Warfare department (300 energy — the most expensive unlock) and completing research on the Research Ship.

On offence, you can disrupt individual enemy drift fields, drain their energy generation, siphon their void matter reserves, blind their radar, or — at the top of the tree — shut down their command ship entirely for 20 seconds. A fully invested cyber player can dismantle an enemy drift field network without firing a single shot.

On defence, when the enemy launches a cyber attack on you, an alert fires and you get a response window — a quick button-mash or key-sequence minigame. Succeed, and you reduce or negate the attack. Fail, and it lands at full strength. Higher-tier defences give you more time, a chance to auto-block, or even reflect the attack back at the enemy.

The tradeoff: the full cyber tree costs 265 RP. That’s RP not going to dampener research, weapons upgrades, or fleet-wide armour. Going all-in on cyber is a commitment — but if your enemy isn’t prepared for it, it’s devastating.

Opening Strategies

At mission start with 1,000 ore, 60 RP, 0 energy, and 0 void matter, you face genuine choices:

  • Economy Rush — Enable Mining Operations first, get harvesters and enticers running immediately. Ore and void matter flowing fast, but you have no territorial presence and no drift fields early. You’re betting on out-producing the enemy
  • Drift Field Rush — Enable Engineering & Prospecting first, research drift projectors, and start claiming hexes using your starting ore. Fast territorial control, but you’re burning reserves with no income to replenish them. If the enemy disrupts your void matter pipeline, your network collapses
  • Turtle — Enable Defence Industry first, mount turrets and shield generators on your capital ships, and weather early aggression. You survive, but you fall behind on territory, economy, and tech. This works as a reaction to early pressure, not as a long-term plan
  • Cyber Play — A long-game investment. Get Mining Operations running for income, then pour everything into the Cyber Department and its research tree. Devastating in the late game, but you’re vulnerable and under-teched for a long time

The 60 starting RP is enough to do one thing well — not everything. That’s the tension, and it’s there from the first second.

RTS — Battlefield Mechanics

Space strategy games have a terrain problem. Without hills, chokepoints, or rivers, battles devolve into blobs smashing into blobs in an empty void. Asteroids only get you so far. We wanted something better — mechanics that create real tactical depth without breaking the fiction of open space.

Saturation Fields

Invisible energy-drain zones seeded across the map by generators. Any ship — ally or enemy — that enters a saturation field has its energy slowly sapped. They won't show up on your main view, only on radar, so there are no glowing boundary boxes cluttering your screen. You'll need detector ships to scout safe routes, and smart players will bait enemies into fields they don't know are there.

The result: space has structure.

  • Invisible on the main view — no glowing boxes, no immersion-breaking overlays
  • Visible on radar only — rewarding players who check their instruments
  • Detector ships required — scouting becomes a real tactical role
  • Affects all ships equally — your own fleet isn't immune, so placement is a double-edged sword
  • Creates corridors and danger zones — flanking matters, recon matters, the map has shape without a single wall

Orbital Hot Zones

The battle takes place in high orbit — far enough from the planet that your fleet can manoeuvre freely, close enough that orbital positions matter for bombardment and landing operations. The planet itself arcs slowly across the bottom of the map on its orbital path, disappearing off one side and re-emerging from the other.

This changes everything. Orbital capture points — the on-ramps for bombardment runs and landing craft drops — shift in value as the planet drifts. A position that's critical right now becomes worthless in a few minutes as the planet moves out of range. Defenders can't just park on a point and hold it. Attackers can't wait forever to push.

Both players see the same orbit. Both can plan around it. The question is who reads the map better.

  • Planet arcs across the map in real time — a slow, visible orbit that reshapes the battlefield
  • Capture points shift in value — a key position now might be worthless in minutes
  • No static defence — defenders have to move with the zones or lose relevance
  • Bombardment windows are temporary — time your push or miss your shot
  • Predictable, not random — both sides have the same information, rewarding planning over reaction

Hex Fields

The missing piece. Saturation fields give space its terrain. Hot zones give the map a heartbeat. Hex fields give it territory — a living, shifting front line that both sides are fighting to control.

The Grid

At mission start, the entire map is divided into a honeycomb of hexagonal zones. This grid is invisible until a hex scout is deployed into a cell, at which point that hex activates — visually distinct, owned, and projecting modified physics constants within its boundaries. The grid is programmatic: calculated from map width and height at load time with a configurable hex_diameter (default 700). No overlap, no manual placement in the editor. Hex scouts snap to the nearest unoccupied hex centre on deployment.

The whole map is claimable. Every hex is a potential battleground.

What Hex Fields Actually Do

Hex fields aren't walls. They don't block movement. Any ship can fly into any hex, friendly or hostile. What changes is the physics inside.

A friendly hex modifies local constants in your favour — better shield regeneration, faster weapon cycling, improved sensor range. The specifics depend on what's been researched and what tier of energy is powering the field. An enemy hex does the opposite: your ships are debuffed, their ships are boosted. Neutral hexes have no effect.

The result is layered, asymmetric defence without a single physical barrier. Pushing through one enemy hex is manageable. Pushing through three in a row, each stacking debuffs, is a meatgrinder — and that's the point. It replaces the “break the wall, break the turret, break the base” progression of traditional RTS with something that fits open space: each hex you cross tips the odds further against you until you crack the network.

  • No movement restriction — any ship can enter any hex
  • Buff/debuff zones — friendly hexes boost your ships, enemy hexes punish them
  • Stackable depth — multiple hex layers create the layered defence that replaces walls and turrets
  • Researchable effects — what each hex actually does depends on your tech progression
  • Scalable by energy tier — a hex powered by void matter gives a modest buff; one running on resonant essence is significantly stronger
Chain Logic

Hex fields don't operate in isolation. Every active hex must be able to trace an unbroken chain of friendly hexes back to a command ship. The command ship is the power source. If the chain is broken — because a hex scout was destroyed or shut down — everything beyond the break goes dark. No buff, no debuff, just neutral space.

This is where the strategy lives. A perfectly built hex network can be collapsed by a surgical strike on a single junction hex. A wide, shallow network is resilient but weak. A narrow, deep one is powerful but brittle. Players have to decide how to shape their network based on the tactical situation, and the enemy is always looking for the load-bearing hex to knock out.

  • Hexes chain back to command ship — no chain, no power
  • Junction hexes are high-value targets — destroying one can cascade a whole section dark
  • Network shape is a strategic choice — wide and resilient vs deep and powerful
  • Flood-fill adjacency check — each hex knows its neighbours; power propagation is a simple connected-component check from the command ship outward
Void Matter Economy

Hex fields consume void matter to stay active. Not a one-time build cost — an ongoing drain, every tick, for every powered hex. Expand too fast and you'll bleed your reserves dry. Lose your harvesters and your network starts collapsing from the edges inward as you're forced to shut down fields to keep critical ones online.

Each hex scout has a manual on/off toggle. Mid-battle, you're managing a power grid under fire — shutting down rear hexes to keep the front line lit, or going dark on a flank to consolidate resources for a decisive push. The more territory you hold, the more it costs to hold it.

This is the longevity mechanic. Battles aren't just about who has more ships. They're about who can sustain their territorial control while disrupting the enemy's supply chain. Hit their void matter harvesters and watch their hex network flicker and die, hex by hex, as they scramble to decide what to sacrifice.

  • Ongoing void matter cost per active hex — expansion has a price
  • Manual on/off toggle per scout — triage decisions in real time
  • Resource pressure creates natural ebb and flow — overextension is punished, efficiency is rewarded
  • Harvesters become strategic targets — choke the supply, collapse the network
  • Mirrors the galactic-scale entropy economy at a tactical level
Reading the Board

A well-designed hex battlefield tells a story at a glance. Your hexes glow one colour, the enemy's another, neutral space is dark. You can see where the front line is, where the gaps are, where a chain is thin enough to cut. The enemy's command ship sits behind layers of hostile hexes. Your job is to find the weak point, punch through, and collapse their network from the inside.

Missions can start with pre-established hex networks — the enemy already dug in, your fleet arriving and deploying its initial hexes as crew chatter contextualises what's happening. No tutorial screen. The player sees the board, hears the team react to it, and understands. Push blue into red. Cut the chains. Take the command ship.

The Payoff

Three systems, one dynamic battlefield. Saturation fields give space its terrain. Orbital drift gives the map a heartbeat. Hex fields give it a front line. Every match plays differently not because of randomness, but because the geometry is always shifting under your feet — and the best commanders are the ones who see it coming.

Critical Build Chains

Chain 1: Hex Scout (Territory Control)

The primary path — getting hex fields online to claim territory:

  1. Deploy Command Ship
  2. Enable Nanite Forges → energy starts generating
  3. Wait for 200 energy
  4. Enable Mining Operations
  5. Build Constructor (100 ore from starting reserves)
  6. Build Harvester (150 ore) → deploy to harvest zone → ore income starts
  7. Wait for 200 energy
  8. Enable Engineering & Prospecting
  9. Research Hex Scout (30 RP from starting 60)
  10. Build Hex Scout (500 ore)
  11. Deploy Hex Scout to hex cell → consumes void matter

Void matter fork: Before step 11 works long-term, you need enticers:

  • After step 6, also:
  • 6b. Build Enticer (200 ore + 15 RP from starting 60)
  • 6c. Deploy Enticer to null zone → void matter starts flowing

Players who rush hex scouts without establishing void matter income will watch their fields flicker off. That's the trap, and it's a good one.

Starting RP budget (60): The player can afford the hex scout research (30 RP) AND one enticer (15 RP) from starting reserves with 15 RP left over. This means early decisions matter — do you spend that remaining 15 on a second enticer for safety, or save it for something else? Once starting RP is spent, further RP requires deployed surveyors.

Chain 2: Dampener Ship (Hex Assault)

The path to breaking through enemy hex networks:

  1. Research Ship: Research “Dampener Field Theory” (40 RP)
  2. Research Ship: Research “Dampener Ship Blueprint” (30 RP)
  3. Assault Carrier: Build Dampener Ship (300 ore)
  4. Deploy with assault fleet → reduces enemy hex debuffs in local radius

Total cost: 70 RP + 300 ore. This is a mid-game investment — you won't have 70 RP to spare early. The player has to decide when to pivot from expanding their own hex network to building the tools to crack the enemy's. Push too early without dampeners and your fleet melts in enemy hexes. Wait too long and the enemy network becomes impenetrable.

Chain 3: Cyber Strike (Disruption)

The path to disabling enemy systems without a direct assault:

  1. Wait for 300 energy
  2. Enable Cyber Department on Command Ship
  3. Research Ship: Research “Cyber Warfare Suite” (40 RP)
  4. Select target → launch cyber attack
  5. Enemy gets chance to respond (button-mash defence)

Why it's late-game gated: 300 energy is the most expensive department unlock, and the research prerequisite means you're investing RP that could go to dampeners or upgrades. Cyber is the “I've stabilised my economy and hex network, now I want to start dismantling theirs from a distance” play.

Chain 4: Full Defence Lockdown

For players who want to turtle and weather early aggression:

  1. Wait for 200 energy
  2. Enable Defence Industry
  3. Build Field Generators (100 ore each, tack onto ships)
  4. Build Turrets (200 ore each, tack onto ships)
  5. Optional: Build Arc-Defence Ships (400 ore)

Tradeoff: You're safe, but you have no ore income beyond starting reserves, no RP generation, and no hex presence. The longer you turtle the further behind you fall on territory. Works as a response to early aggression, not as a long-term strategy.

Capital Ships (Reserve Ships)

Capital ships are distinct from assault carrier production. They are persistent — they survive between missions, carry upgrades forward, and grow in power over the campaign. Think pets, not cattle.

  • Persist across missions — damage, upgrades, and loadout carry over
  • Individually upgradeable — RP and void matter spent on a specific capital ship, not fleet-wide
  • Limited in number — you won't have many, each one matters
  • Boss-tier combat presence — significantly stronger than standard fleet units
  • Named and characterful — tied to the narrative, crew dialogue, and story progression

Capital ship upgrade details will be fleshed out as the system develops. The key constraint: RP and void matter spent on capital ships is RP and void matter NOT spent on fleet-wide unlocks, hex fields, or cyber operations. Every resource in this game should hurt to spend.

Dark Worlders / Drone Fleet

Originally known as the Dark Worlders by the ancient Skrill race. The Sekarri refer to them as the Drone Fleet to reduce panic, as the name "Dark Worlder" carries a legacy of hopelessness.

Origins

Proto-Human in origin — the original humans, grey in colour, from another galaxy. They once had nations, many races and colours, but eventually formed a single totalitarian government and a single skin colour: grey. As they expanded outward, they were intensely xenophobic.

They originated on a planet over 1 billion years ago. Originally a civilization with culture and wars similar to others, they became obsessed with technological supremacy, eventually utilizing eugenics and total domination. They were eventually trapped on a planet surrounded by a Null Field — a barrier that prevented matter from existing.

Visual Evolution

  • Early Expansion: Their army and politicians looked mechanised — giant armoured suits, faces concealed, breathing mask fittings. Like Vorlons from Babylon 5.
  • Mid-Expansion: Their form shifted from metallic to wizard-like: robes, cloaks, facial masks became capes. Front line troops looked like mages.
  • Late Expansion: Ethereal, light-bending material. Face obscured to prevent aliens looking upon the face of their "gods."

The Skrill War & The White Hole Event

An ancient race called the Skrill (Keepers of the Universe) broke the barrier, believing they could "fix" the Dark Worlders' programming. This acted as Pandora's Box. Upon release, the Dark Worlders destroyed the Skrill's 10th Fleet and ignited a 10,000-year war.

The Skrill attempted a diplomatic meeting near an entropy sink (black hole) to contain the threat. The Dark Worlders betrayed them, corrupted the entropy sink, turning a black hole into a White Hole. This rift allowed reinforcements from another galaxy, leading to the extinction of 95% of all life in the galaxy.

Hierarchy

  • Arkons: Regional/Galactic political masters. Visible as orbs of energy with no physical body. They oversee harvesting quotas, entropy conversion, and construction of exotic artefacts.
  • Sovereigns: Supreme military commanders charged with conquering entire galaxies. Appear as mages in cloaks wielding "magical" staves (high-technology weapons). Cannot be killed and can spawn like instances of an app.
  • Arch-Pestel: Brigade/corps leaders who micromanage at the solar system/planetary level. Charged with ensuring the expungement of 95% of populations. Now exist as part of the Sovereign's mind.

Military Capabilities

According to Kira, no race has ever won a single skirmish against them. Their technology appears as "magic" to the Sekarri (who are 300 years ahead of humans). They have wiped out 95% of tier 2 and 3 sentient life in the galaxy.

The "Hornet" Strategy: They utilize scouts. If a scout is alerted, the fleet swarms in overwhelming force. The only counter-measure is to destroy or distract the scout instantly. The Sekarri once sacrificed an entire fleet just to stop a scout from reporting their location.

// Science-Lore

Entropy

In short: Sovereigns harvest life in the galaxy to extract entropy (crystallised/locked information). The entropy can be used to create Fake Universes, in which they build things they need. They then import those things using Virtual Entropy.

Entropy is a base layer of reality. The real-world definition is the measure of the unavailability of energy to do work, or a measure of disorder. Drone Commander expands this interpretation as the percentage of locked-in information, unmodifiable — essentially, the soul.

Entropy is superior to available information, in that entropy can be used to generate new information. This is what makes it so valuable. The Dark Worlders have limits on what they can create (stars, black holes, etc.) as physics limits what information can do. Entropy allows them to work around this — a minable resource giving access to exotic matter like Shard Strange Matter.

Casting Alternate Fake Universes

The Dark Worlders treat entropy like an oil rush. They don't care that harvesting extracts souls or destabilises local space — it powers their economy, allowing them to make Fake Universes, Shards, and cosmic-constant altering matter.

Alternate realities don't exist in Drone Commander. However, the top-tier species can manipulate and cast alternate probabilities into micro-universe bubbles within our existing universe. With Fake Universes they can build whatever they want; with Virtual Entropy, they import what they built.

The Dark Worlders believe they are doing good, because they think they can prevent the universe from ending by leaching Fake Universes.

Entropy Spawn

  • The leftovers of when entropy is harvested or corrupted. Must be processed to form into Spawn.
  • When a forge is created and people (alive or dead) are brought for processing, wastage is left over that festers into malicious energy.
  • The Dark Worlders realised that if left untouched the energy would dissipate, so they channel it into Entropic Spawn — a second layer of defence for the forges.

Starlanes, Shards & FTL

It's not faster than light, nor transportation, nor quantum nonsense.

Billions of years ago, species noticed that not all CMBR (Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation) was equally distributed — it appeared to clump together in 12 places. On closer inspection, CMBR seemed to almost instantly appear in another location. There were 12 regions in the galaxy where physics behaved differently.

The top races of that era began a gold rush, racing to each location and bottling up the super-resource into Shards. Shards are like batteries for special matter that can change the physical constants of the universe.

  • If you have a shard, you don't need to play by the rules of the universe
  • You could double your weight or make the speed limit 5x faster
  • You could make distances shorter
  • But like oil, you have to refine it for what you want — there are hundreds, maybe thousands of shards

The shards are bottled super-physics from regions of early CMBR where physical constants were different and variable. These clumps of space have long dissipated, but the ancients raced to mine, refine and purpose them. Different shards have different functions related to the part of space they came from.

History of the Sekarri (Sekar venatrix)

Venatrix meaning gender imbalance. Sekarri come from a wider family of Sekar, meaning smart hunters.

Pre-History: During their last major evolutionary phase ~200,000 years ago, environmental factors caused females to die at much higher rates in childbirth. Through selective breeding and survival of the fittest, women who bore more females were selected for — creating the 7:1 forcing function.

Early History: Social bonding dominated their civilisation. The instability of breeding meant many Sekarri still carry epigenetics from previous Sekar favouring monogamous relationships. It took the rule of law, religion and even caste systems to engrain their polygamous society. A Pride System was formed.

< 800 Years Ago: Sekarri maintained strict traditions — any male not pairing with more than 6 women would be severely punished, as would women who attempted to keep a man to herself.

After 400 Years Ago: Living standards improved. Autonomy and individual rights became more of a thing. Cultural revolutions followed. People were free to form relationships how they pleased — with the final caveat being that high-level government officials must still be seen to follow traditions.

To adapt to the gender imbalance, Sekarri females developed fine needle-like hairs below the belly button that stand on end during arousal. These inject a form of organic hormones into males on contact — a genetic historical adaptation.

Resonant Essence

Just as humans once knew of light speed but believed in the Ether before Einstein showed the true 4-dimensional nature of the universe, the Vionites have glimpsed the basic principles of entropy in action. They understand this as Resonant Essence.

This discovery took 200 fleet ships working for 300 years in one of the most extreme environments known — a pulsar system. The combination of time-dilation, intense radiation, and the gravity gradient allowed them to view how information works at the fundamental level.

700 years ago, the Vionites came across several dead Honnehari corpses in a Void Stasis Krypt. General Ozerath's staff, working with a Honnehari Eridix (Ethereal AI/droid), were able to reverse-engineer ancient, godlike Tier 3 technologies. This hinted at Resonant Essence.

After centuries of work, they finally built instruments to measure Resonant Essence. They expected to see a new field, particle, or dimension.

They did not expect to see the fundamental soul data of living beings.

Vionites, Sekarri, humans — all complex, branching data snippets that grow, split and merge. Not DNA code created from parents, but immortal data streams that resonate through different dimensions and time domains. Even after death, their Essence directly influences the evolution of the galaxy.

This shocked the Vionites to the core. The pragmatic conclusion: they were at least 100,000 years behind from being able to make Tier 3 status. The system is now off-limits, but like Area 51, the technology benefits are being slowly siphoned to the populace — including Stellar Architecture via resonant stream fields.
